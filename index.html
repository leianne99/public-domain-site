What is the Public Domain?

The public domain is a collection of creative works that are no longer protected by intellectual property laws such as copyright, trademark, or patent. These works are considered public property, meaning anyone can use, reproduce, modify, or distribute them — without needing permission or paying royalties.

Works enter the public domain when:

  • Copyright has expired (usually 70 years after the author’s death)
  • The creator has waived their rights
  • It was never eligible for copyright protection (e.g., facts or formulas)
  • It is a government publication

Examples of Public Domain Works

Literature

  • Pride and Prejudice by Jane Austen
  • The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes by Arthur Conan Doyle
  • Noli Me Tangere by Dr. Jose Rizal

Music

  • Beethoven’s Symphony No. 9
  • Mozart’s compositions
  • Traditional folk songs

Art

  • Mona Lisa by Leonardo da Vinci
  • The Creation of Adam by Michelangelo

Government Works

  • Philippine Constitution
  • Supreme Court rulings
  • Public advisories by government agencies

Why the Public Domain Matters

The public domain keeps knowledge, culture, and creativity accessible to everyone.

1. Education

Students, teachers, and researchers can access books, documents, and images freely for learning.

2. Innovation

Creators can remix and transform old works into something new without legal restrictions.

3. Preservation

Important cultural works — from Beethoven to Rizal — are preserved and passed down to future generations.
